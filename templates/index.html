A website for newcomers to Formula 1 (F1)

Official F1 site

What is F1?

Formula 1 is the pinnacle of motorsport. Unlike other series (like Indycar, Nascar, or Forumla 2), F1 is not a spec series - every car is different, and they are developed to suit each teams' drivers' bodies and driving style. The FIA - F1's governing body - sets technical rules and regulations for car development. Within those rules, teams are allowed to modify their cars to make them faster, or more reliable. Ideally, they try to do both.

What's at stake?

The Formula 1 World Championship. Actually there are two championships at play each season: the World Drivers' Championship, and the World Constructors' Championship

Who participates?

Racing in F1 is not for everyone. The amount of resources a team needs, and the skill level drivers need make it a highly exclusive club to join. Only 10 teams are currently racing in F1 (as of {{simple_date}}) and they get to vote on whether another team is allowed to enter.
Each team is allowed two drivers on track at any given time, be it practice sessions, qualifying, or the Grand Prix. Most teams employ no more than three drivers: two main drivers, and a reserve/testing driver who sometimes travels with the team, and sometimes stays in the factory testing upgrades and setups in the simulator (yeah, really cool stuff)

Key concepts

  • Grand Prix: the main event of every race weekend. Takes place on Sundays, mostly in the afternoon, sometimes in the evening. The number of laps is dictated by length of the track: the total race distance must be at least 300km, so the lap count will be the minimum number required to reach that, plus one lap. Monaco is the exception.
  • Sprint: a short race held on several occasions per season. Sprint races must be 100km in length, and the lap count is calculated the same way it is for the Grand Prix. Sprints take place on saturdays.
  • Downforce: the opposite of lift, downforce is generated by the wings, floor and bodywork of the car when travelling at high speed. It helps the car stick to the track, allowing to take corners at great speed. It also causes the cars to generate up to 5G of lateral forces, comparable to those experienced by fighter jet pilots.
  • Drag: when going in a straight line, drag is the air pulling back on the car and slowing it down. It's directly correlated to downforce: the more downforce you generate, the more draggy the car is. Teams will try to fine tune the car setup for each track during Free Practice sessions in order to maximize performance in the corners without being too slow in the straights.
  • Car setup: the combination of front wing angle, rear wing angle, ride height, suspension stiffness, wheel camber and toe, and suspension geometry, among other things. The perfect balance should make the car drivable in slow, medium, and high speed, fast in the corners and in a straight line, and minimize lap times and by extension race time. Fuel load is also a factor.
  • Dirty air/Slipstream: when following a car in a straight line, slipstream is the gap behind the leading car where there is less air, having been pushed out by the wings and bodywork. Chasing cars in the slipstream gain a top-speed advantage.
    The opposite is true when following through corners: less air in front of you means you don't generate the required downforce to keep the speed through the corner, thus you are slower.
  • DRS - Drag Reduction System: because following can be tough with dirty air, chasing cars less than 1 second behind can use a system in the rear wing to reduce drag in the straights. Throughout each track there are DRS detection points - some grant DRS over multiple zones. When a driver crosses them 1 second or less behind another car, they can press a button in their steering wheel that opens a gap in the rear wing. This reduces drag and grants a top-speed advantage. DRS automatically shuts off when pressing the brakes.
  • Tyres: F1 works with Pirelli to deliver 6 compounds of tyre in varying degrees of softness. The softer a tyre compound, the faster but less durable they are. The harder the tyre compound, the slower but more durable they are. Each race weekend, Pirelli selects three compounds that will be available to the teams. Of these three, every driver is required to use two over the duration of a Grand Prix, forcing them to stop. This does not apply to races where wet-weather tyres are in use: if rain is present, they are not required to stop.
  • Race strategy: F1 cars cannot hold enough fuel to run flat out the entire race, and they are also not allowed to refuel during the race. So drivers will often drive slower on purpose to save fuel and make it to the end. Likewise, they don't want to make more pit stops than necessary. So they go slower to manage tyre temperatures and degradation. But for some teams, pitting more often and using the extra grip, or taking a gamble with a safety car, might be worth it. Teams will devise several possible race strategies ahead of the race to account for all possibilities, and will execute them as needed.
  • Safety Car/VSC: if there's an incident on track, be it a car crash, cars retiring, debris, or un-drivable conditions, a Safety Car or Virtual Safety Car may be deployed. The Safety Car will go around and wait for the leader. Until then, drivers must slow down and be prepared to stop. Once it has picked up the leaders, all drivers must queue up behind. Lapped cars may be allowed to overtake when the incident is cleared, and will (almost always) be waited on before restarting the race. VSCs are similar with the exception of backing up the pack: it doesn't. The VSC is a time to which drivers must adhere, about 40% of full speed. This maintains the gaps between drivers and can be deployed and recalled instantly.
  • Delta: used to refer to the time difference between two times. Normally, drivers will see their delta between their fastest lap and their current lap. Sometimes they prefer the last lap and the current lap. During VSC periods, they'll use the VSC time, but in this case they'll want their delta to be positive (losing time) but as close to 0 as they can get it.
