About F1 Participants

  • The Drivers

    The drivers in F1 have to be the best in the world. Not only because they are piloting race cars capable of reaching speeds upwards of 220mph, but because they have to fight for their spot. Because each one of the 10 teams can only have 2 drivers, there are always more capable drivers than available seats, and it is their performances and skills that dictate who gets a drive.
    On top of that, F1 drivers must acquire an FIA SuperLicense by racing (and succeeding) in other series in recent years.

  • The Teams (Constructors)

    Each of the 10 F1 teams has to be willing to spend the big bucks to go for glory.
    As in, to have a go. Only one team can end the season as the best team on the grid, and someone must also be last, so there's bound to be losses for a lot of teams.
    Reality is a bit different though. Since 2019, the Haas team has finished no higher than 8th, Williams have finished last 3 times, Alfa Romeo finished 8th, 9th, 9th, and 6th (and are currently 9th) and AlphaTauri (Toro Rosso in 2019) have fallen from 6th in 2019 and 2021, to 9th in 2022 and currently last.
    It's easy for F1 teams to get stuck in these negative loops unless they have the money to spend on the car, which these teams do not.

What about engines?

The foundation of a race car (or any car for that matter) is the engine. Without one, it doesn't matter how aerodynamic your chasis, skilled your driver, or speedy your pit crew, you simply won't leave the garage. But not every team can afford to build an engine (or knows how to... really).
Some teams will build their own engines, and some will purchase their engine from other teams

  • Ferrari

    As the longest running team in F1, and one of the most prestigious sport car manufacturers in the world, Ferrari have the knowledge and capital to build their own race engine. It brought them success in the beginning of the century thanks to Michael Schumacher (2000-04) and Kimi Raikkonnen (2007) early in the decade thanks to Sebastian Vettel (2010-13), back when Red Bull got their engines from Ferrari.
    In recent years, however, they haven't really been able to replicate their results, having had the worst engine on the grid in 2020. They've recovered since, and are still looking to mount a proper challenge for the title.
    Ferrari supplies engines to Haas and Alfa Romeo

  • Mercedes

    Germans can build cars and engines. And Mercedes-Benz is no different. Although they are a relatively newer team, having been bought after the 2009 season, they've found success thanks to Nico Rosberg (2016) and Lewis Hamilton (20014-15, 20017-20). While their engine may not be the strongest on the grid anymore, it is certainly is quite strong still, and very reliable as well. Mercedes supplies engines to McLaren, Aston Martin, and Williams

  • Honda/Red Bull PowerTrains

    Bet you didn't know Red Bull made engines... sort of.
    Back in 2018, they were using Renault engines, but the poor results that season caused them to look elsewhere. Being the owners of the AlphaTauri (then Toro Rosso) team, Red Bull had positive experiences with Honda, and they decided to go all in, almost becoming a Honda-works team.
    After two seasons of unreliability, Red Bull's gamble paid dividends thanks to Max Verstappen (2021-22) winning back-to-back championships and Red Bull (2022) winning the constructors to end Mercedes' era of domination. Not only that, but they had become the smallest (and therefore lightest), most powerful, most reliable engine
    As Honda decided to pull out of F1 at the end of 2021, Red Bull established their new department: Red Bull PowerTrains, essentially taking over from Honda with the manufacturing, maintenance, and modifications of their engines.
    But make no mistake, that's a Honda engine under that RBPT badge.
    Red Bull supplies engines to their sister team AlphaTauri

  • Renault

    Renault have not raced in F1 since the end of 2020, and yet their engines are still used by... oh
    THE FRENCH
    Renault F1 Team became Alpine F1 Team at the start of the 2021 season, but both being French companies (and owned by the French people) it meant but a cosmetic change: new liveries, new team uniforms, new driver overalls, new merchandise, and a new name. The engine, however, is still made by Renault for Alpine, but they are a works team for all intents and purposes.
